Mapping MU's Enrollment

The state's flagship university experienced at 20 percent enrollment decline between fall 2015 and fall 2016. According to MU, reasons for the decline include the fall 2015 protests, fewer high school graduates in Missouri, and greater competition from other universities. But the decline has shown a spotlight on demographic divides on campus. While taxpayers across the state help fund MU, most of the Missouri residents who attend the university are white and from urban areas.

By Ruth Serven, The Missourian | May 8, 2017

Diversity across the state

On average, black residents make up about 12 percent of Missouri's population and 13 percent of its high school graduates (Missouri's non-white graduates make up a smaller percentage than in other states in the region, however). But in most of the state, black residents make up much less of individual counties' population. Numbers do not include St. Louis City residents, which are 48 percent black.

Decline in total undergraduates, by county

In the fall of 2015, MU had about 17,000 in-state undergraduates. A lot of the declines were in rural counties, but counties surrounding urban areas like St. Louis and Kansas City also sent fewer students to the university.

Decline in first-time students, by county

First-time enrollment from rural counties declined sharply across the state, by as much as 100 percent in some counties. Overall, the fall of 2016 saw about 20 percent fewer freshmen than previous years. On Friday, MU announced that it expected its 2017 freshman class to shrink even further, by about 15 percent.

Demographic changes

As millennials graduate college, the class of high school graduates will get smaller and smaller. Across Missouri, the age cohorts of people 0-17 and 18-24 have shrunk over the past few years. The Western Interstate Commission for Higher Education predicts that this lull in Missouri's high school graduates, down from a high of 71,000 in 2010, will continue until 2021. This decline is not just a problem in Missouri. According to a 2015 report, the number of new college students was 1.7 percent lower than in the previous year.
